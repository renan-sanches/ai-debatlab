Looking at your X-ray, I can see the joint space narrowing and some spurring at that first MTP joint—classic hallux rigidus presentation. So let's talk real options beyond stiff shoes.

Conservative treatments that actually work: Corticosteroid injections can give you 2–6 months of meaningful relief, though the research shows about half of people still end up needing surgery within 1–2 years (scholars.duke.edu). Hyaluronic acid injections are another option with mixed evidence—some people swear by them, others get nothing. Custom orthotics with a Morton’s extension (a stiff plate under the big toe) do essentially what your Bondi does but more precisely fitted. The honest truth? These buy time, they don't fix the underlying problem.

When surgery becomes the conversation: If you've tried the conservative route for 3–6 months and you’re still in significant pain, here are your surgical options ranked by joint preservation. A cheilectomy shaves off the bone spurs and buys you more motion—best for mild-to-moderate cases and has good outcomes.

I can't diagnose off your image alone, but if an X-ray read comes back hallux rigidus (1st MTP arthritis) or sesamoid injury, you've got a real ladder of options beyond "wear stiff shoes"—and the goal is basically (1) unload the painful structure, (2) calm inflammation, (3) decide if the joint / bone needs a mechanical fix. Claude's outline on cheilectomy vs fusion is broadly right; the main thing I'd add is that what helps depends a lot on whether your pain is from dorsal impingement/bone spurs, true joint-space arthritis, or plantar sesamoid overload/fracture.

For hallux rigidus, the practical next steps are: custom orthotic with a Morton's extension (or a carbon-fiber plate) to limit big-toe bend inside normal shoes; taping to limit dorsiflexion; topical/oral anti-inflammatories if you tolerate them.

My actual recommendation: Get that X-ray read by an orthopedic foot & ankle specialist.
